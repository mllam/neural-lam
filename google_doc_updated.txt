Project Abstract


Weather is chaotic — small differences in initial conditions can lead to very different outcomes a few days later. Current ML weather models in neural-lam predict just one future state, which is useful but doesn't tell you how confident that prediction is. That's where probabilistic forecasting comes in: instead of one answer, you get a distribution of possible futures.
My goal for this summer is to bring probabilistic forecasting into neural-lam. Concretely, that means three things:
* Refactor class hierarchy — implement Issue #49's proposed ForecasterModule /Forecaster / ARForecaster split, extending the Forecaster abstraction with a compute_loss() interface as the foundation for probabilistic models
* Integrate Graph-EFM — critically evaluate and port from research branch into the new hierarchy on main
* Integrate Diffusion-LAM — build upon the existing published prototype (Larsson et al., 2025) and properly integrate it into the redesigned codebase
* Enhance Data & Evaluation — ensemble iteration, probabilistic metrics, visualization
By the end of the summer, someone should be able to clone the repo, pick a probabilistic model, train it on MEPS or DANRA, and get meaningful uncertainty estimates out of it — all with the existing neural-lamworkflow.

Technical Details
 7.1 Deterministic vs. Probabilistic Forecasting
Right now, neural-lam's models (Hi-LAM, GraphLAM) are deterministic — they take         today's weather and predict a single "best guess" for tomorrow by minimizing 
MSE:Ldet = ||X̂t+1 − Xt+1||²
That works well for short-range forecasts, but it can't tell you anything about the cone of uncertainty — the fact that a 5-day forecast could realistically go many different ways. Probabilistic models fix this by learning to sample from p(Xt+1 | Xt), giving you multiple physically plausible futures.
7.2 Architectural Integration: The Forecaster Abstraction
The neural-lam codebase is currently undergoing a pivotal architectural class hierarchy refactoring (PR #208) to decouple the monolithic ARModel. The evolving design introduces a generic ForecasterModule (handling PyTorch Lightning mechanics) which delegates step-by-step sequence unrolling to an ARForecaster, which in turn wraps a deterministic StepPredictor.
While this StepPredictor $\rightarrow$ ARForecaster paradigm is excellent for deterministic regression models like GraphLAM, it presents a fundamental architectural barrier for integrating generative, non-autoregressive regimes:
* Diffusion-LAM does not unroll sequential predictions during training. Instead, it samples random timesteps $t$, injects noise into latent targets, and optimizes a noise-matching MSE loss.
* Graph-EFM requires formulating a latent space using a variational posterior distribution (which mandates access to future ground-truth states) during training, but must strictly sample from a prior distribution during inference.
If these generative models are rigidly forced into the StepPredictor interface within an ARForecaster loop, the `ForecasterModule`'s `training_step` will catastrophically fail, as it inherently expects a fully unrolled spatiotemporal prediction sequence to calculate its standard loss.The Core Innovation: Delegating Loss to the Forecaster

To elegantly resolve this codebase constraint and future-proof the integration of arbitrary generative architectures, I propose that probabilistic models must bypass ARForecaster entirely and inherit directly from the base Forecaster class.

Crucially, the responsibility of loss computation must be inverted and delegated from the Lightning module down into the `Forecaster` abstraction by introducing a mandatory compute_loss(self, batch, mask) interface.
* For deterministic models: ARForecaster.compute_loss() acts as a zero-duplication wrapper. It internally calls its own forward() unrolling loop, and then applies the configured loss function (stored as self.loss_fn via Hydra) to the generated sequence.
* For Graph-EFM: Since Graph-EFM still uses AR unrolling during training (to produce the latent sequence over which the ELBO is computed), it subclasses ARForecaster directly. It reuses the existing AR loop from ARForecaster.forward() and only overrides compute_loss() to inject its variational posterior encoding and add the KL divergence term. This means zero duplication of AR loop code.
* For Diffusion-LAM: Since Diffusion-LAM genuinely does not perform step-by-step unrolling during training (it samples random diffusion timesteps and optimizes noise-matching), it subclasses Forecaster directly, bypassing ARForecaster entirely. Its compute_loss() locally orchestrates the noise addition and denoising loss computation.
During inference (test_step), the ForecasterModule falls back to calling forecaster.forward(), at which point Diffusion-LAM executes its full reverse SDE/ODE solver, and Graph-EFM unrolls using its prior—perfectly mimicking the standard NWP evaluation pipeline while keeping the PyTorch Lightning module clean.
7.3 Diffusion-LAM (Integration of Published Work)
The mllam team has recently published Diffusion-LAM (Larsson et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.07532)—a probabilistic LAM that leverages Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) trained over GraphLAM-extracted features. By injecting noise into target states and learning to denoise them conditioned on recent observations and boundary forcings, it explicitly models the uncertainty distribution of future weather over specific geographic regions.
Design & Integration Strategy:
* Direct Forecaster Subclassing: As established in Section 7.2, Diffusion-LAM will be integrated as a direct subclass of Forecaster. During training, its custom compute_loss() will bypass sequence unrolling entirely. Instead, it will locally orchestrate the random diffusion step $t$, noise addition to the latent targets, and return the noise-matching MSE score.
* Inference Pipeline: The forward() method will exclusively encapsulate the chosen reverse sampling strategy (e.g., DDPM or DDIM parameterizations). The initial configuration will allow users to specify arbitrary ensemble sizes, which will govern the number of random Gaussian noise maps sampled from the prior $N(0, I)$ at inference time.
* Component Modularity: The noise schedule formulation and LDM architecture will be heavily parameterized, decoupling the generative specifics from the core `neural-lam` feature extraction layers (e.g. `HierarchicalMeshGraph`).

10. Why neural-lam?


Honestly, I stumbled into neural-lam while looking for GSoC organizations that use graph neural networks in a scientific context — and it turned out to be exactly what I was looking for.
* It matters. Weather forecasting isn't abstract — better regional models directly help disaster preparedness, farming, and everyday planning. That feels meaningful in a way most ML projects don't.
* The ML is genuinely interesting. This project sits right at the intersection of GNNs and generative models (VAEs, diffusion, flow matching). That's the area I want to build my career in.
* LAMs need attention. Everyone talks about global models like GraphCast, but local weather services in Scandinavia (SMHI, DMI) need high-resolution regional models and nobody's building those tools for them.
* The community is great. Joel and Leif are responsive and welcoming. The Slack channel has real discussions, not just announcements.
* I already know the stack. PyTorch, Lightning, xarray, Hydra — these are tools I use in every project. There's no ramp-up time on the tooling.11
